How to read this — and important caveats
  • Dots are California high schools. Dot size encodes the denominator of the metric you're viewing — applicants for admit rate, admits for yield, A–G eligible for per–eligible rates, grade 9–12 enrollment for per–head rates, cohort for A–G completion — so a bigger dot always means a larger base for that rate. Only schools at or above Min applicants are shown (a fixed sample floor, kept on applicants whatever the size metric). Scale ranges are padded by the largest dot radius so whole dots stay inside the frame.
  • Reading the rates. All outcome rates are shown as proportions (an admit rate reads 0.241, a yield 0.45) so the eight funnel ratios share one scale; the academic/context measures (CAASPP, A–G completion, UPP, ELWR) stay as the percentages they natively are. The admit rate is to the selected campus only (admits÷applicants there), not a UC system–wide rate.
  • “Mean across schools” (the dashed line and headline figure) is the arithmetic mean of the per–school rates — the central tendency of the dots, which is what this tool measures. The separate Student–weighted figure is the pooled Σnumerator÷Σdenominator rate, dominated by large schools and shown only for reference; the LCFF+ gap is likewise a difference of school–level means.
  • Year / period. Pooled periods sum the funnel across their years; single years show one cycle. Every rate is computed as a ratio of sums over years where both numerator and denominator are observed, so it is comparable across periods of different length.
  • Explore the cloud. Scroll to zoom, drag to pan, double–click to reset — handy for spreading piled–up dots enough to click one. Focus mode (top–right) hides the control bar and side panels for a clean, full–width view. You can divide any funnel stage by A–G eligible or by grade 9–12 enrollment via the metric menu.
  • Drop outliers. Ticking the box removes, for the current view, any school flagged by the standard Tukey 1.5×IQR rule on either plotted axis (the single metric in strip view). Dropped points leave the chart, the correlation r, the trend line, the mean and the LCFF+ gap; the number removed is reported. Because the headline mean is outlier–sensitive, this is the intended way to set extremes aside.
  • Base counts. A selected school's profile lists the raw denominators behind its rates — A–G eligible, grade 9–12 enrollment and graduating cohort — and every rate row also shows its own numerator ÷ denominator.
  • Cross-universe ratios. Applicant/admit/enrollee counts come from UC (CEEB); “eligible” (A–G completers) and enrollment come from CDE (CDS). Ratios mixing them are school-level indicators, not exact per-student rates.
  • Self-selection. A negative merit–admit correlation does not by itself prove a school-level penalty; the over-time view and within-LCFF+ comparisons are the checks against it.
  • Suppression & reliability. UC masks small admit/enroll cells; at Berkeley ~a third of applicant schools have suppressed admits. When the numerator is masked in the years carrying most of a school's applicants or admits, a pooled rate would otherwise collapse onto a small surviving year (e.g. an apparent 1.00 yield, or a 0.50 admit rate from 3 of 6 in one year while 31 applied over the period). Such rates are marked low-confidence: drawn as hollow dashed dots, excluded from the headline r, the mean and the LCFF+ gap, and shown in a school's profile as their actual numerator ÷ denominator with the number of suppressed years.
  • Strip position. In the strip view each dot sits exactly at its value on the axis; only vertical spacing is used to separate overlapping schools, so a dot's horizontal position always matches the number in its profile.
  • Newcomer schools. A few schools for recently arrived immigrant students (e.g. Oakland International) show ~0% CAASPP proficiency while still posting A–G completers — students meet graduation requirements while acquiring English. That 0% is real data (it may round from a small masked percentage), not a missing value.
  • Impossible denominators. A handful of schools have a corrupted grade 9–12 enrollment far smaller than their applicant count (e.g. Wheatland). Rows where a funnel count exceeds total enrollment are dropped from the “÷ enrollment” metrics rather than plotted as >1.
  • Capacity dynamic. High-admit inland campuses (Merced, Riverside, Santa Cruz) show a positive merit–admit correlation — they admit most qualified applicants — so read them differently.
  • ELWR met at entry. The writing–requirement figure is the share of a school's UC enrollees who satisfied the Entry Level Writing Requirement at entry — via the AWPE exam or other qualifying methods (AP/IB/SAT/ACT/transferable course) — i.e. who did not need first–year remedial writing. It is not “ever met”: enrollees only (self–selected, small N), latest year per school, indicative only.
  • SAT / ACT. Where available a profile shows the school's UC–enrollee average SAT reading (EBRW, 200–800) and ACT (writing–section takers, 1–36) for the latest year on record. These exist only for ~2006–2020 (none after 2020), are enrollee–only, small, self–selected samples — context, never a merit axis. SAT “writing” appears only on its old 200–800 section scale, as the post–2016 essay is not comparable.
  • Applicant & admit GPA. The two GPA axes are UC's own recalculated weighted, honors–capped high–school GPA (sophomore–junior A–G coursework — the figure UC uses in admissions), averaged over the selected period. Applicant GPA gauges the strength of a school's UC applicant pool: an applicant–specific merit axis, distinct from CAASPP (which covers all grade–11 students). Admit GPA is the average among admitted students — the GPA bar UC actually accepted — and is partly downstream of the admit decision (a more selective campus shows a higher bar), so read it as a level UC set, not a pure measure of school strength. A profile also shows enrollee GPA and the admit−applicant gap (how far above the pool the admitted students sat). Admit GPA is blank wherever UC suppresses a school's admits.
